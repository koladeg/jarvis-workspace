# Orisynx Conflict Resolution Framework

Adapted as a reusable internal decision-disagreement template for Orisynx, inspired by Matter's decision-disagreement framework reference shared by Kolade:
https://matterapp.com/blog/decision-disagreement-framework-how-we-encourage-disagreements-at-matter

## Purpose

Use this when:
- founders or team members disagree on a product, technical, operational, or ownership decision
- tension is building around a repeated issue
- a discussion is going in circles
- a decision needs to be made without damaging trust

This is **not** for emergencies, misconduct, harassment, or legal/safety issues. Those should be handled separately and immediately.

---

## Core Rules

1. **Assume good intent first**
   - Start from: "we are trying to solve the same business problem."
2. **Disagree on the issue, not the person**
   - Attack assumptions, tradeoffs, timelines, risks, and evidence.
   - Do not attack motive, intelligence, or character.
3. **Write before arguing endlessly**
   - If a disagreement lasts more than a short live discussion, move it into the template below.
4. **The decision owner must be clear**
   - Not every decision is democratic.
   - But every meaningful decision should have input, reasoning, and a visible outcome.
5. **Commit after decision**
   - Once the decision is made, everyone aligns in execution unless the decision is reopened under the rules below.
6. **Reopen only with new evidence or broken assumptions**
   - Not just because someone is still unhappy.

## Decision Types at Orisynx

Before resolving the conflict, classify the decision:

### Type A — Founder / strategic
Examples:
- company direction
- equity-sensitive decisions
- governance
- major partnerships
- high-level product positioning

**Default owner:** founders collectively

### Type B — Product / customer / workflow
Examples:
- audit workflow design
- permission model behavior
- approval process
- UX priorities

**Default owner:** product lead or assigned founder, with team input

### Type C — Technical / implementation
Examples:
- architecture choices
- database modeling
- frontend/backend implementation path
- deployment choices

**Default owner:** technical lead or directly responsible engineer

### Type D — Operational / process
Examples:
- meeting cadence
- task tracking flow
- review structure
- communication norms

**Default owner:** whichever founder or lead owns operations for that area

If ownership is unclear, first assign a temporary decision owner before debating substance.

## Fast Meeting Version (15–20 minutes)

Use this when the disagreement is important but should not become a long drama.

1. **State the decision in one sentence**
2. **Give each side 3 minutes uninterrupted**
3. **List shared facts**
4. **List actual disagreement points**
5. **Name the decision owner / decision rule**
6. **Choose now or define a short experiment**
7. **Document the outcome in writing**

If a live discussion becomes emotional or repetitive, pause and move to the written template.

## Escalation Ladder for Orisynx

### Level 1 — Direct resolution
Two people try to resolve it directly using the template.

### Level 2 — Owner call
If unresolved, the assigned decision owner makes the call after hearing both sides.

### Level 3 — Founder review
If the decision has strategic, trust, governance, or major company-wide impact, founders review it together.

### Level 4 — Trial / experiment
If the disagreement is about prediction rather than principle, run a time-boxed test.

Example:
- try one workflow for 2 weeks
- compare delivery speed, bug rate, and user clarity
- decide based on results

### Level 5 — External input
If the team lacks expertise or neutrality, bring in a trusted external advisor.

## Rules for Healthy Disagreement

Do:
- be specific
- bring evidence
- separate fact from fear
- speak early before resentment builds
- document decisions
- challenge ideas honestly

Do not:
- use group chat for emotional ambushes
- relitigate closed decisions without new evidence
- recruit allies privately just to "win"
- turn execution issues into character judgments
- hide behind vagueness

---

## Recommended Orisynx Norms

1. **For meaningful disagreements, write it down**
   - If it affects roadmap, architecture, roles, delivery, or founder alignment, it should exist in written form.
2. **Use owner-based decisions for speed**
   - Everyone gives input; not everyone gets veto power.
3. **Use experiments when prediction is the dispute**
   - The cleanest way to end many arguments is to test.
4. **Keep a decision log**
   - Store major decisions and disagreement outcomes so the same issue is not re-fought repeatedly.
5. **Review patterns, not just incidents**
   - If the same kind of conflict keeps happening, the real issue may be unclear roles, weak process, or missing trust.

## Simple Default Script

When tension starts rising, say:

> Let’s stop arguing loosely and run this through the Orisynx conflict template.
> What exactly is the decision, who owns it, what are the options, and what evidence would change our minds?

That line is usually enough to shift the conversation from friction to structure.
